Threat to Democracy? How Brazilian Politicians Approached January 8th on Twitter

Only 32% of the tweets published by parliamentarians on the day of the attacks mentioned their consequences for Brazilian democratic system

By Rafaela Sinderski

Photo: Joedson Alves/Agencia Brasil

On January 8, 2023, more than 4,000 Brazilian citizens invaded and destroyed the administrative buildings of the federal government in the country's capital, Brasília. The headquarters of the Executive, Legislative, and Judiciary branches were attacked by the crowd that did not accept the results of the 2022 presidential elections.

These acts were the culmination of a series of political demonstrations that took over the country since last October, when the far-right former president Jair Bolsonaro (PL) lost the electoral race to the leftist candidate Luiz Inácio Lula da Silva (PT). The results reflected the polarized political landscape: Lula had a narrow victory with 50.8% of the valid votes, leading him to his third term as Brazil’s president and causing the wave of violent and anti-democratic outbreaks.

As the episodes unfolded, many political actors used their Twitter profiles to comment on the attacks. Lula published a thread criticizing the Institutional Security Office's performance in protecting government facilities and promising to punish the "financiers of those who went to Brasília." Bolsonaro, on the other hand, used Twitter to criticize Brazil's current president and the supporters of the political left-wing. He also denied his connection to and influence on the anti-democratic events.

- Manifestações pacíficas, na forma da lei, fazem parte da democracia. Contudo, depredações e invasões de prédios públicos como ocorridos no dia de hoje, assim como os praticados pela esquerda em 2013 e 2017, fogem à regra.

— Jair M. Bolsonaro (@jairbolsonaro) January 9, 2023

(Peaceful political demonstrations, in the form of the law, are part of democracy. However, depredations and invasions of public buildings as occurred today, just like those practiced by the left in 2013 and 2017, escape the rule.)

The former president's speech echoed among some of his supporters within the Brazilian National Congress; they reproduced the defensive posture and the attempt to transfer responsibility to other political groups. However, this was not the general tone adopted in the messages published on January 8th. The word cloud below shows that the tone of repudiation prevailed among parliamentarians – from House of Representatives and Senate – who had active Twitter accounts on the day of the attacks. "Vandalism" and "terrorism" were widely used to describe the events.

The word cloud is based on 444 tweets published by Brazilian National Congress members on January 8th.

There are minimal differences in the use of terms considering the political group to which parliamentarians belong – on the right, in the center, or on the left of the political-ideological spectrum.

To the left: focus on 'terrorism'

Politicians aligned with the left wing have used the word "terrorist" extensively to refer to January 8th. Their tweets also mentioned Jair Bolsonaro and his supporters (called "bolsonaristas") more frequently, seeking to hold the former president responsible for the attacks on the federal capital.

Word cloud made with tweets from leftist politicians..

To the right: vandals with no names

Parliamentarians who identify with the political right wing preferred "vandalism" to "terrorism" when discussing the anti-democratic acts. They did not mention the far-right former ruler or his "bolsonaristas" followers. Even though they repudiated the violence that took Brasília by storm, they talk about punishment in a more generic and non-directed way.

Word cloud made with tweets from right wing politicians.

Politicians at the center of the spectrum behaved more closely to right wing parliamentarians, using the term "vandalism" and avoiding mentioning political actors and supporters by name.

Word cloud made with tweets from politicians at the center of the political-ideological spectrum.

Although "democracy" is a term present in the word clouds, the lexical analysis made from the tweets of Brazilian Congress members revealed that only 32% of their publications on January 8th mentioned the term.
